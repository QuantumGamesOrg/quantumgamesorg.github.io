The number of red panels on Alice's detector is always even

The number of red panels on Bob's detector is always odd

The single lighted panel common to both detectors always has the same color on both

The source S periodically sends out particles to Alice and Bob that reach them simultaneously.

Alice chooses one of the rows R1, R2 or R3 of her detector to fire and Bob one of the columns C1, C2 or C3 of his. They do this independently and randomly, just before the particles arrive.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen row of Alice’s detector is always even.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen column of Bob’s detector is always odd.

The one common panel that lights up on both detectors always has the same color on both.

The pattern of lights observed on the detectors illustrates the phenomenon of quantum entanglement, which is a correlation between events at distant locations that cannot be traced back to a common cause in the past.

This demonstration is linked closely to the game Peres-1, although the connection is not obvious.

Peres-Mermin Magic Square

The source S periodically sends out particles to Alice and Bob that reach them simultaneously.

Alice chooses one of the rows R1, R2 or R3 of her detector to fire and Bob one of the columns C1, C2 or C3 of his. They do this independently and randomly, just before the particles arrive.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen row of Alice’s detector is always even.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen column of Bob’s detector is always odd.

The one common panel that lights up on both detectors always has the same color on both.

The pattern of lights observed on the detectors illustrates the phenomenon of quantum entanglement, which is a correlation between events at distant locations that cannot be traced back to a common cause in the past.

Click on “Explanation” to learn how this demonstration reveals entanglement.

This demonstration is linked closely to the game Peres-1, although the connection is not obvious.

Explanation

It seems reasonable to assume that the detectors are triggered by the particles they get from the source. If they are triggered by some other agency, then that agency must play the role of the particles in the explanation below.

There is no telling what the common panel on the two detectors will be in any run, since Alice and Bob make their choice of detector settings independently (and just before the particles arrive!). The common panel could therefore easily be any of the nine.

But if the common panel is always to light up the same color on both detectors, the particles going to them must ensure that their corresponding panels always light up the same colors (so that no matter what the common panel is, it always lights up the same color on both).

Call the common colors assigned to the panels of the two detectors in any run an instruction set. Then the particles going out from the source must convey identical instruction sets to both detectors in any run.

Because each row of Alice’s detector is required to have an even number of red panels, the total number of red panels in her instruction set must be even.

But each column of Bob’s detector is required to have an odd number of red panels, so the total number of red panels in his instruction set must be odd.

However, the total number of red panels in one and the same instruction set cannot be both even and odd, showing that viable instruction sets accounting for all the features of this demonstration simply do not exist.
