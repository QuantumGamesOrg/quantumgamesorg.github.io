The source S periodically sends out particles to Alice and Bob that reach them simultaneously.

Alice chooses one of the rows R1, R2 or R3 of her detector to fire and Bob one of the columns C1, C2 or C3 of his. They do this independently and randomly, just before the particles arrive.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen row of Alice’s detector is always even.

The number of panels that light up red in the chosen column of Bob’s detector is always odd.

The one common panel that lights up on both detectors always has the same color on both.

The pattern of lights observed on the detectors illustrates the phenomenon of quantum entanglement, which is a correlation between events at distant locations that cannot be traced back to a common cause in the past.

Click on “Explanation” to learn how this demonstration reveals entanglement.

This demonstration is linked closely to the game Peres-1, although the connection is not obvious.
